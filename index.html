Understanding Rainfall Trends

Across Australia, average rainfall has declined from around 3.3 mm to 2.5 mm between 2007 and 2017.
This shift may seem small, but it reflects broader changes in regional weather systems and the hydrological cycle.
Rainfall is one of the most visible indicators of Australia’s climate balance and while it is not the only factor influencing bushfires, declining precipitation contributes to drier vegetation and soil, which together create the conditions for larger and more frequent fires.
Tracking rainfall over time helps us identify how Australia’s changing climate is reshaping both ecosystems and fire risk.

Changing Temperatures in Australia

Since the 1960s, Australia’s surface temperatures have climbed steadily, with recent decades showing the sharpest rise on record.
This long-term warming trend is consistent with global patterns driven by greenhouse gas accumulation in the atmosphere.
The impacts are visible everywhere, from record-breaking heatwaves to altered rainfall timing and reduced water availability.
As climate change continues, the question is no longer whether human activity contributes, but how effectively we can adapt and reduce our collective impact.
Australia’s response today will shape its environmental resilience for generations to come.

Growing Fires in Australia

Between 1990 and 2020, the total land area burned by bushfires has nearly doubled.
While fire is an essential and natural part of many Australian ecosystems, the frequency and intensity of recent fire seasons suggest that climate conditions are amplifying their scale.
Hotter, drier years combined with prolonged drought and reduced rainfall are transforming once-cyclical fire events into climate-driven crises.
This raises an important question: are we witnessing a new normal in the Australian fire landscape, or the consequence of decades of unsustainable emission trends and land-use pressure?

Australia in the Global Climate Balance

The map of recent fire intensities reveals a troubling pattern, large portions of rural and agricultural Australia are repeatedly affected by severe bushfires.
These areas form the economic and ecological backbone of the nation, and while urban centres may remain relatively insulated, the damage to regional livelihoods, biodiversity, and air quality is profound.

When we broaden our view globally, the story becomes even more complex. Despite having a smaller population, Australia ranks among the world’s highest CO₂ emitters per capita, placing it alongside major industrial nations such as the United States and China.
Raw emissions figures can obscure this imbalance, once normalised per person, Australia’s footprint highlights the challenges of a resource-intensive economy and its reliance on carbon-heavy energy production.

Among domestic contributors, the electricity, gas, steam, and air-conditioning sector stands out, producing more emissions than the next nine industries combined. This dominance reflects the country’s continued dependence on fossil fuels for power generation.
Transitioning toward cleaner, renewable energy sources represents both a significant challenge and an enormous opportunity, one that will determine whether Australia can continue to thrive economically while playing its part in stabilising the global climate system.
